Choosing a Class

Core Classes

The standard classes -- recommended for first time players.

  • Barbarian -- A high damage melee class with the ability to rage to lower defenses and increase damage. Main Stats are Strength and Constitution.
  • Bard -- A support class that uses music and performance to weaken enemies or buff their allies. Main stats are Charisma and Dexterity.
  • Cleric -- The classic healer, gaining power from their god to heal their party members, as well as turn themselves into effective tanks. Main stats are Wisdom and Charisma.
  • Druid -- An all-around class, capable of being a spell-weilding damage dealer, a melee fighter through transformation, or a supportive healer. Main stat is Wisdom.
  • Fighter -- A combat based class that able to excel in offensive, defensive, or ranged combat. Main stats are Strength or Dexterity and Constitution.
  • Monk -- A melee fighter who, rather than use weapons, fights with their bare hands to unleash a rapid number of attacks in a short time. Main stats are Strength, Dexterity, and Wisdom.
  • Paladin -- A combination of Fighter and Cleric, capable of providing healing while similtaniously functioning as a defensive force. Main stats are Strength and Wisdom.
  • Ranger -- A guardian of nature focused on either two-weapon fighting or using a bow and arrow for precise attacks in combination with nature magic. Main stats are Dexterity and Wisdom.
  • Rogue -- A heavy damaging warrior who fights from the shadows, usually requiring the help of other fighters to reach their full power. Main stat is Dexterity.
  • Sorcerer -- A mage born with the ability to cast magic; can deal high damage, but doesn't have much in the way of defense. Main stat is Charisma.
  • Wizard -- A mage who attained magical ability through study; deals heavy damage, but doesn't have much in the way of defense. Main stat is Intelligence.

Base Classes

Simple bonus classes -- can be played by first time players, but better experienced on a first or second character.

  • Alchemist -- A master of potions of various effects, transformations to change their stats, and throws bombs to control the battlefield. Main stats are Dexterity and Intelligence.
  • Cavalier -- A fighter than battles from atop a mount, leading their allies into battle by increasing morale. Main stats are Strength and Dexterity.
  • Gunslinger -- A ranged warrior that covers the field with a constant barrage of attacks, with high damage and health. Main stat is Dexterity.
  • Inquisitor -- A religious zealot that roots out the cause of chaos in the world, using divine magic alongside melee combat. Main stats are Wisdom and Strength.
  • Magus -- A mage who, rather that using their magic to attack, uses magic to boost their melee weapons to deal massive damage. Main stats are Intelligence and Strength or Dexterity.
  • Oracle -- A divine healer born with their powers; a sort of cleric with the spellcasting style of a sorcerer. Main stat is Charisma.
  • Summoner -- A magic being who uses their magic to create a powerful creature that fights in their stead. Main stat is Charisma.
  • Vigilante -- A type of rogue with a social and vigilante personality that allows them to control the city in the day, and the streets at night. Main stats are Dexterity and Charisma.
  • Witch -- A dark magician who curses her foes and blesses her allies with unknown magicks. Main stat is Intelligence.

Alternate Classes

Slightly different versions of some of the preivous classes, created for those who want minor changes or alternative versions.

  • Antipaladin -- A paladin, but rather than a bastion of goodness, a force of evil and destruction. Main stats are Strength and Wisdom.
  • Ninja -- A rogue, but with an emphasis on acrobatics and poison use. Main stat is Dexterity.
  • Samurai -- A cavalier, but instead of a mount, there's a larger focus on leadership through increasing the party's morale. Main stats are Strength and Constitution.

Hybrid Classes

Classes focused on mixing two other classes into a new and unique class. Often some of the most complicated classes to play.

  • Arcanist -- The ultimate mage, combining aspects of Sorcerer and Wizard to be powerful, yet versitial. Main stat is Intelligence.
  • Bloodrager -- A combination of Barbarian and Sorcerer; they use their blood to empower themselves physically while also utilizing bloodmagic. Main stats are Strength, Constitution, and Charisma.
  • Brawler -- A combination of Monk and Fighter, constantly changing their fighting style to accomadate for any situation. Main stats and Strength and Dexterity.
  • Hunter -- A combination of Druid and Ranger, with slightly less combat abities than ranger, but far more magical prowess. Main stats are Dexterity and Wisdom.
  • Investigator -- A combination of Alchemist and Rogue, the Investigator studies his opponents to allow himself to deal massive damage. Main stat is Intelligence.
  • Shaman -- A combination of Oracle and Witch, using the holy power of the Oracle and the dehabilitng powers of Witches. Main stat is Wisdom.
  • Skald -- A combination of Barbarian and Bard, combining the incredible damage-dealing abilities of the Barbarian with the buffing power of Bards. Main stats are Strength and Charisma.
  • Slayer -- A combination of Ranger and Rogue, tracking down foes and finishing them off with massive killing blows. Main stat is Dexterity or Strength.
  • Swashbuckler -- A combination of Fighter and Gunslinger, engaging foes in melee combat and using skills to deflect damage and respond with massive counterattacks. Main stats are Dexterity and Charisma.
  • Warpriest -- A combination of Cleric and Monk, using the healing powers of a cleric with the attacking abilies of a monk.

Choosing a Race

  • Human -- The most typical race, effective at playing almost any class.
  • Elf -- A classic elven race; boasts incredible dexterity and intelligence, while also frail creatures, with a low constitution.
  • Half-Elf -- Half-Elf, and typically half-human as well, half-elves gain some of the benefits of being elven, while keeping the ability to be effective at any and all classes.
  • Dwarf -- Stoudy and sturdy, Dwarves are naturally wise creatures, though typically not Charismatic. This makes them natural choices for front-line warriors.
  • Halfling -- Based on the classic hobbits, Halflings are quick and Charismatic creatures that are naturally less strong than their larger allies. They make natural warriors based off of speed.
  • Gnome -- Sturdy, loveable, and physically weak, Gnomes are incredibly small, but creative and eccentric beings.
  • Half-Orc -- Incredibly large, bulky, and usually harsh, Half-Orcs are usually excluded by society -- but their incredible strength makes them powerful warriors.

Feats

  • General Feats -- The general feats include a number of feats that help to improve your character in general ways, usually statistically or outside of combat, allowing for more creative roleplaying abilities, and skill bonuses. In addition, many of the feats allow you to gain more abilities relative to your class -- extra abilites or per day use of class skills. For skill focused classes, Skill Focus is good for giving a massive increase to one skill in particular. Most the feats are specific, and should only be used to supplement onto other feats and skills, rather than standing on their own.
  • Combat Feats -- Combat feats are where the most useful feats are often found; some are even nearly required for playing certain play styles. Some important feats include;
    1. Power Attack -- This allows take a minus to your to hit in order to deal massive damage; exceptionally good for fighters, particularly two-handed weapons, such as Greatswords or Greataxes.
    2. Two-Weapon Fighting -- This allows you to wield two weapons at once, at a small minus to each of their attacks.
    3. Weapon Finesse -- This allows you to add your Dexterity Modifer to your to hit roll instead of your Strength bonus, making Dexterity based builds possible.
    4. Combat Reflexes -- This allows you to make multiple attacks of opportuntiy a round, forcing your opponents into staying in one place, and making them easier to surround.
    5. Point-Black Shot -- This is the start of a chain of ranged attacks that improve ranged attacks and remove many of the penalties of range.
    6. Weapon Focus -- This gives you a bonus to a particular weapon, and can be combined with feats such as Weapon Specialization to become devestating with a particular weapon -- particularly as a Fighter.
  • Item Creation Feats -- Item Creation feats are incredibly complicated, and shouldn't be used for a first time campaign. The basic principle is that you give up a number of funds and xp in order to create powerful items, often requiring a magical background and a number of invested feats.
  • Metamagic Feats -- Metamagic feats work differently for all magic classes, but the shared factor between all the metamagic feats is that you use a higher level spell slot to cast a lower level spell with empowered effects.
  • Racial Feats -- Racial feats are small bonuses that are only available to certain races and often increase the number of times a race-based ability can be activated.
  • Feat Tree -- This feat tree includes all feats, and also shows what feats lead towards other feats, and is a good resource for planning a character that you'll be playing over an extended period of time.

Skills

Skills are abilities that all players have -- some just have more than others. Based on your class, you'll have a number of class skills, or skills that come natural to your character. When you use a skill point in a class skill, you immediately gain a +3 bonus to every check made with that skill -- so particularly early game, your class is almost half of what skills you end up using. Every level, you get a certain number of skill points, effected by your class and your intelligence bonus. For example, a Fighter only gets 2 skill points plus their intelligence bonus each level, where a rogue gets 8 plus intelligence -- giving rogues significantly more flexibility in the skills they choose to specialize in. You can read in detail about skills in general here.

There are a number of skills, each with their own uses in and out of combat. A lot of the time, it's up to the player to elect to use their skills, and sometimes the DM will ignore certain skills altogether; I personally pay no need to the Diplomacy skill, instead forcing my players to convince the NPC they're speaking to through role-playing; but every DM will be different, and it's good to know what your DM's opinon on certain skills are.

The different skills are;

  • Acrobatics -- This allows your character to nimbly jump around the battlefield, giving you the opportunity to avoid Attacks of Opportunity with successful saves.
  • Appraise -- This allows your character to identify the value of any item; the better you do, the more specific you are. Check with your DM to see if this is a mechanic they plan to use.
  • Bluff -- This allows your character to better weave lies, forcing the target of a lie to make a Sense Motive check against you. Check with your DM to see if this is a mechanic they plan to use.
  • Climb -- This allows your character to climb any surface physically climbable. The more difficult the climb, the higher the DC.
  • Craft -- This allows your character to become an expert in building a certain type of item. Read up on craft to learn more about the different items that can be built, and discuss the uses of the items with your DM.
  • Diplomacy -- This allows your character to make enemies more friendly towards it, as well as barter down prices. Check with your DM to see if this is a mechanic they plan to use.
  • Disable Device -- This allows your character to pick locks, disable traps, and subtly break items around the world (to your DM's discretion).
  • Disguise -- This allows your character to disguise themselves as another person -- often with the help of a disguise kit.
  • Escape Artist -- This allows your character to free themselves from bonds or grapple checks; this is incredibly useful for small melee fighters who don't have a high CMD (see combat section).
  • Fly -- This allows your character to better control themselves while flying. Naturally, only useful to certain characters; most of the standard races can't fly without magical means.
  • Handle Animal -- This allows your character to befriend animals in the wilderness, and calm wild animals.
  • Heal -- This allows your character to cure poisons, bleed effects, and stabilize dying allies. It does NOT allow you to regenerate you or your allies hit points.
  • Intimidate -- This allows your character to attempt to intimidate their foes, lowering their offenses and defenses. There's quite a few feats and class abilities that expand on Intimidate.
  • Knowledge -- Knowledge is a broad section used to analyze monsters weaknesses and resistances, as well as learning facts about surrounding areas and locals in the world. Knowledge types and what they cover include;
    • Arcana -- Magic Traditions, Arcane Symbols, Constructs (except Robots), Dragons, Magical Beasts
    • Dungeoneering -- Aberrations, Caverns, Oozes, and Spelunking
    • Engineering -- Buildings, Aqueducts, Bridges, Fortifications, Robots, Identifying Non-Magical Items
    • Geography -- World layout(Maps), Terrain, Climate, Who Lives in an Area
    • History -- Wars, Colonies, Migrations, City History; varies in use based on the DM's world, and how they plan to give information
    • Local -- Legends, Notable Personalities, Laws, Customs, Traditions, and analyzing Humanoid enemies
    • Nature -- Animals, Fey, Monstrous Humanoids, Plants, Vermin, Weather Patterns
    • Nobility -- Lineages, Heraldry, Notable Personalities, Royalty, Royal Customs
    • Planes -- The Planes of Existence, Outsiders, Planar Magic
    • Religion -- Gods, Goddesses, Mythic History, Holy Symbols, Undead
  • Linguistics -- This allows your character to learn new languages -- used at the DM's discretion.
  • Perception -- This allows your character to investigate their surroundings; to loot dungeons, find traps, hear enemies moving behind a closed door -- whatever you and your DM choose.
  • Perform -- This allows your character to learn some form of arts, and can often perform in public to try and earn more gold.
  • Profession -- This is your character's 'real' job; can be used effectively with a character's backstory to make them feel like a real person, rather than just a professional goblin killer.
  • Ride -- This allows your character to ride on any manner of beasts.
  • Sense Motive -- This allows your character to try and pick up on other characters bluffs, or try and pick out someone's motivation. Check with your DM to see if this is a mechanic they plan to use.
  • Sleight of Hand -- This allows your character to hide weapons or pickpocket.
  • Spellcraft -- This allows your character to begin to identify different types of magic, often to try and glean the power behind a rival mage or the nature of a magic item.
  • Stealth -- This allows your character to attempt to remain hidden from sight, usually rolling against an enemy's perception.
  • Survival -- This allows your character to survive in the wilderness, as well as allowing them the ability to track quarry of any nature over long distances.
  • Swim -- This allows your character to (get this) swim.
  • Use Magic Device -- This allows a non-magical character to use magic items that they would otherwise not be able to use (such as wands, rods, etc.).

Equipment

Naturally, everyone needs equipment to make their characters function; armor to keep the character alive, and weapons to help deal damage, as well as much more -- depending on how the DM chooses to run things. If they want to go fully realistic, the Pathfinder system goes into full detail on rations and carry weight. Other than that, equipment is a key part of any Dungeons and Dragons campaign.

  • Weapons -- No character is complete without a sword in hand; there are plenty of weapons to look through, each with their own special and unique abilites.
  • Armor -- Not every character will use armor, though; in return for a boost to your Armor Class, you often loose a small amount of mobility, gain some difficulties casting certain types of magic, and can't use Dexterity quite as much. However, with the right character, a good set of armor can make the difference between life and death.
  • Goods and Services -- Some DM's don't want to deal with this section, but it can allow for some fantastic roleplaying and creativity. Rope, food, clothing, disguise kits, lockpicking tools; everything an adventure would want besides weapons and armor is found in here.
  • Special Materials -- Certain monsters have damage resistance to most weapons, with usually one weakness. Werewolfs, for example, take reduced damage from everything not made of silver. To create a weapon or a set of armor or something unique, check through these pages to find what the additional requirements of the items are.
  • Magic Items -- By far the most complicated section of equipment, Magical Items fall under one of two categories. The first is a general magical enchancement, usually put on weapons and armor to increase the basic effect of the item. Second are specific magic items, of which there are two many to count. As your level increases and you gain power, magic items are all but required to keep the characters powerful enough to face off against some of the more powerful monstrosities in the world.

Magic

Not every classes uses magic, and every class that does uses it differently, and usually has a different list of spells and abilites they can draw from. Information about the general rules of how magic works can be found here.

  • Bard and Skald Spells -- Bards and Skalds know a certain number of spells by heart, and can pull out of this list of known spells a certain number of times per day.
  • Cleric Spells -- Clerics must pray to their god every morning and choose exactly what spell will be in what slot; if they ask for two hold person, they only have two hold person. The one catch is that any spell can be exchanged at will for a Cure or Inflict Wounds spell, based on the Clerics alignment. Warpriests share the Cleric's spell list, through only up to Level 6.
  • Druid Spells -- Druids meditate to create a list of pre-determined of spells each day. They technically know every spell, but must select uses whenever they rest. Hunters also share in this spell list, but only with spells up to Level 6.
  • Paladin Spells -- Paladins function much like clerics, but their spell list is significantly more limited.
  • Ranger Spells -- Rangers function much like Druids, but their spell list is significantly more limited. Hunters also share in this spell list.
  • Sorcerer and Wizard Spells -- Sorcerers, Wizards, and Arcanists all share this spell list, but all have a different way of casting spells. Wizards prepare each spell and the number of uses from a spellbook, Sorcerers know a certain number they pull from at will, and Arcanists prepare spells, but not the number of uses.
  • Alchemist and Investigator Formulae -- Alchemists and Investigators, rather than casting spells, brew potions with a certain number of different effects that mimic spells. Naturally, the potions have be prepared before casting, usually during a rest.
  • Inquisitor Spells -- Inquisitors know a certain number of spells by heart, and can pull out of this list of known spells a certain number of times per day.
  • Magus Spells -- Magus must study their spell book and plan what spells to prepare and the number of times to prepare them, mimicking a wizard.
  • Oracle Spells -- Oracles know a certain number of spells by heart, and can pull out of this list of known spells a certain number of times per day.
  • Summoner Spells -- Summoners know a certain number of spells by heart, and can pull out of this list of known spells a certain number of times per day.
  • Witch Spells -- Witches must meditate every morning, choosing what spells to prepare for the day, and the number of times to prepare them.
  • Antipaladin Spells -- Antipaladins function much like clerics, but their spell list is significantly more limited.
  • Bloodrager Spells -- Bloodragers know a certain number of spells by heart, and can pull out of this list of known spells a certain number of times per day.
  • Shaman Spells -- Shamans must meditate every morning, choosing what spells to prepare for the day, and the number of times to prepare them.

Bestiary

This section is only for the Dungeon Master, or anyone interested in looking at stats. Though making every creature your group is going to fight is a possibility, Pathfinder has thousands of differnt monsters already designed and balanced for parties still fighting a small group of goblins, to parties killing Satan himself. The monsters are ranked by race, as well as Challenge Rating, also known as CR. For reference, the CR is meant to represent what level a group of four standard adventurers will need to be to fight one of these monsters. For example, A Common Basilisk is CR 5, which means a party of 4 players should be able to fight the Basilisk without being too overwhelmed, but without just waltzing through it. Based off of your party's composition and stats, as well as your player's experience in combat, the amount of difficult enemies you'll be able to throw at them will change -- always be aware of your party's strength, and read up on the monsters you plan to use ahead of time.

Some monsters are certainly easier to use than others -- here's a recommendation of one monster in particular from every CR that I find works well for a beginning party, as well as things to be aware of.

  • Goblin: CR 1/3 -- Goblins make for fantastic enemies for starting your campaign against. They're not too strong, iconic, and are easy to create small and short dungeons to start your party off on their adventure.
  • Ghoul: CR 1 -- A classic undead foe, these zombified beings can be quite difficult for a lew level party -- but can be an intimidating horde at higher levels as well.
  • Bugbear: CR 2 -- Usually the leaders of Goblins and Hobgoblins, Bugbears can function as an excellent first boss encounter for an early party.
  • Hell Hound: CR 3 -- These demonic dogs can help supplement demonic foes when the party is around level 5-7; adding two hell hounds into a boss fight with a more powerful devil can add a massive level of tension into an already insane battle.
  • Mimic: CR 4 -- Everyone loves chests; Mimics are just a way to make the party afraid of even the most simple items. If your party is just smashing through everything, a Mimic might be able to convince them to slow down.
  • Troll: CR 5 -- Trolls are an enormous amount of fun. Classic in fantasy lore, and difficult enemies that require more strategy than just 'stab it until it dies', Trolls are an excellent enemy type.
  • Clockwork Soldier: CR 6 -- These are some of the first major constructs a party can encounter; and they're some of the most vicious. Use this soldier sparringly -- espeically as a guardian of a more powerful mage, a Clockwork Solider is a terrifying creature.
  • Chimera: CR 7 -- Another staple of the fantasy genre, Chimera's a huge, stand-alone monsters that your players feel awesome taking down. It seems unbeatable, but it's a fantastically balanced mosnter.
  • Efreeti: CR 8 -- Efreeti can make phenominal main villains during an early arc of the campaign. Powerful, magical, but not squishy, Efreeti can serve a DM in quite a few ways.
  • Vampire: CR 9 -- Another contender for possible main villain, Vampires are classic antagonists in Dungeons and Dragons games. There's also a template that allows you to make a Vampire of any level -- but the standard model is built for level 9.
  • Rakshasa: CR 10 -- Grotesque, graceful, ruthless, and powerful, Rakshasa are phenominal monsters; they're easy to use in most campaigns, either as a group the party needs to stop, or as the left hand of a mid-game villain.
  • Thunderbird: CR 11 -- Powerful, deadly, and a being of the skies, the Thunderbird is another phenominal creature that can be used as a target for hunting, a creature to save, or whatever else you can come up with.
  • Purple Worm: CR 12 -- A classic Dungeons and Dragons beast, the Purple Worm is a gargantuan enemy, meaning that the party can very quickly feel small. If you want a party to feel like they've killed something incredibly powerful, the Purple Worm is an age old choice.
  • Iron Golem: CR 13 -- Returning back to the world of Constructs, Iron Golems are the basic physical soldiers. Heavy armor, high damage, and difficult to kill, these hulking monstrosities are a ton of fun to use.
  • Demilich: CR 14 -- Demiliches are notoriously known as 'the party killer'. Only use this monstrosity against an advanced or overpowered party, or you'll quickly find yourselves rolling up new characters. However, if you want to eliminate a few characters, the Demilich is the way to go.
  • Ancient White Dragon: CR 15 -- It's called Dungeons and DRAGONS, after all. Though there are dragon ages and types for players of all levels, the Ancient White Dragon is a great being for running across a high level party. Dragon Hunting is difficult, but incredibly fun.
  • Horned Devil: CR 16 -- The heads of armies of devils, Horned Devils are vicious demonic foes that serve as amazing boss fights; there's devils and demons of all levels, but they're incredibly difficult throughout the entire game. Use them carefully!
  • Rune Giant: CR 17 -- Usually, once you're at this high a Challenge Rating, most creatures will be the most powerful thing in the room; a single Rune Giant could take on a Dragon, but add in the tribal nature of these beasts, and even max level characters will struggle with these infamous destroyers.
  • Water Yai Oni: CR 18 -- Going into Eastern fantasy lore rather than Western, Oni are a fantastic way to shake up a campaign. Water Yai are some of the most powerful, but can also make some of the most entertaining antagonists.
  • Shoggoth: CR 19 -- Cthulhu has stats, but trust me when I say that the party's not going to fight him. They can, however, try to take down a Shoggoth. Good luck!
  • Tarrasque: 25 -- The Tarrasque is a one of a kind monster. It only wakes up every few thousand years, has an Intelligence of 2, and is difficult, if not impossible to truly kill. The ultimate killer of parties, the Tarrasque is well-known for being one of the most powerful beings in Pathfinder; short of the gods.
